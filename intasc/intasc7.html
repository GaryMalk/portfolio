InTASC Standard #7
Instructional Planning

The candidate plans instruction that supports every student in meeting rigorous learning goals by drawing upon knowledge of content areas, curriculum, cross-disciplinary skills, and pedagogy, as well as knowledge of learners and the community context.

Reflection

I plan for instruction by considering where we are in the flow of the unit and the course. What background knowledge do we build on from the previous lessons or prerequisite knowledge? What remedial steps will the learner and I take if that knowledge is missing, rusty, or not fully mastered? What are the key learning objectives needed for the upcoming material?

Opening class with a "Do Now" assignment has proved valuable. It is a short, open book, open note, open internet low-stakes quiz on material I haven't lectured on yet. This delivers the messages that from the moment we enter the classroom, we are in a learning space and that students are expected to read the textbook first in preparation. They have the chance to correct their answers, so it's not devastating if they haven't. It also gets their minds engaged on the topic from the moment we start, so they are not just lecture spectators.

On the first day of some classes, the "Do Now" will be what I call the anthropological part of learning any subject, the "History of..." part. I want students to grasp their place in history and to make a commitment to themselves about how much time they are going to devote to this course.

A lecture with presentation graphics typically begins with review, going over the main points of the previous lesson. I'll often do this by asking questions to allow the students to state the points. If this class is the second or later in a series of classes, then at least one day of the first week will be spent going through prerequisite lessons at an accellerated rate. I will use the "Fist of Five" or similar technique to see how confident students are. The plan for the day may change dramatically, if needed.

For coding classes, the lecture usually concludes with a coding demonstration. The code I write is similar to what is required for the assignment, or contains chunks of code that can be copied so it is useful for scaffolding.

During the lab portion, I go around the room and check how students are doing and answer questions. I make sure I spend some time sitting with any students with cognitive issues, assisting them with scaffolding and additional instruction.

When there is not a coding assignment or if it's not a coding class, there is often an Exit Ticket for students to demonstrate they understood the key concepts and give them a chance for reflection.
