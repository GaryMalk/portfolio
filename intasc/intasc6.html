InTASC Standard #6
Assessment

The candidate understands and uses multiple methods of assessment to engage learners in their own growth, to monitor learner progress, and to guide the candidate’s and learner’s decision making.

Assessments either provide feedback as the learner is progressing, contribute to determining how well the learner has mastered the material for the purposes of a grade, or both. Formative Assessments provide feedback to the learner and/or the teacher. Summative Assessments contribute to the student's grade at the end of the process.

Formative Assessments

As an instructor, I assist students with feedback as they are working as well as solicit feedback while I'm teaching. I encourage students to evaluate themselves by providing tools and opportunities to critique each other. Artifacts which demonstrate my use of formative assessment:

  • Fist of Five: this screenshot is inserted at multiple points in a lecture presentation. Students are asked, as a group, to raise their hands in the air with a number of fingers displayed indicating their confidence in the material presented so far. When I see students displaying 0-3 fingers, I stop and probe for what the point of confusion is. When possible, I'll ask a student displaying 4 or 5 if they can explain it. Sometimes hearing it from another student makes all the difference. 
  • Mud Check: asking a yes/no question such as "did everyone understand that" or "are there any questions" will not always get results because sometimes a confused student is afraid of looking stupid. "What is the Muddiest thing I said today" or "what was the most confusing concept for you?" is open ended and presumes that something was confusing which makes it safer for the student to bring something up.

I ask both questions verbally when not using presentation software.

  • Online HTML and CSS validator tools: My HTML students are required to use these on their assignments before turning them in, which makes them formative self-assessment. However, I will use these tools, too, and if I find any errors the tools immediately become summative! We have weekly assignments, so getting points knocked off a previous one is usually, again, a formative experience. There are many online valiator tools, but these are my favorites:
    HTML Validator      CSS Validator
  • Students give each other feedback, such as critiquing each other's Final Projects as displayed in Standard #4..
  • "Do Now" Quizzes: In order to make sure my students are reading the textbook before the lecture, they have a short, low-stakes, open-book quiz at the beginning of class before I've even lectured on the material. It is partly summative because it does contribute to their grade, but the points are low, I will go over the correct answers in the lecture, and students in the lower division classes can take the quiz a second time, so it's mostly formative.
  • Scholarship Essay - written feedback: This was part of the Paying for College unit in the Personal Finance class. I present as artifacts the student's initial submission, my feedback, and the student's final submission.

Scholarship Rough Draft Feedback

Great start, with a moving emotional narrative. A few things:

You've got the vulnerability piece nailed. Now you have to figure out where you are going with this. How is this related to your educational goals and what you want to do with your life? What insights do you have?

Some crafting issues:
"My mom is a hard worker, she’s worked at..."
Those are two independent clauses. They can't be joined by a comma. I would change the first part into its own sentence:
"My mom is a hard worker. She’s worked at..."

"remeber" - spelling: remember

"An empty shell where a man once was." I get your meaning, but that's a judgement. I would leave it out and stick to the facts such as silent car rides, repetitive birthday cards, and not being there.

"For aslong as I can remember, my grandfather has been my bestfriend." I would make this the start of a new paragraph, because it is a change of subject from your mom and dad. Also:

"aslong" that's two words: "as long"

"bestfriend" also two words

"In my darkest days he was what got me through, even now I still do my best for him."

Again, you have two independent clauses joined by a comma. It's much stronger like this:
"In my darkest days he was what got me through. Even now I still do my best for him."

Use grammar check & spell check, and ask at least three people to critique it for you. One of those people should be an English teacher and one should be another student.

Great start, though. You communicate your feelings very well. Tie it in with what you have learned and what you want to do and it will be a strong contender.

Scholarship Final Draft

Most days, the house is loud. Not with laughter or music, but with the sounds of crying toddlers, parents yelling, and the aching reminder of responsibility. I’m the oldest of three, and while most people my age were worrying about what to post, I was figuring out how to clean the whole house, babysit, work, and get my homework done all in the same day.

My mom is a hard worker. She’s worked at the same place for as long as I can remember, 40 hour weeks, sometimes 60. My dad, I don’t even really know. He’s always been in my life but he’s never really been there. Silent car rides, birthday cards that all say the same thing. They always say we’re working on things but nothing has ever changed. 

For as long as I can remember, my grandfather has been my best friend. I used to go to his house every summer and every week or weekend if my parents allowed. He would always tell me his crazy stories while we’d work in the yard and work on all these different projects. I didn’t talk to many people in my life about what was going on with me. A lot of people wouldn’t even understand. He understood me though, he saw the best in everyone. When he passed away a few months ago, I felt like a piece of me had died too. In my darkest days he was what got me through. Even now I still do my best for him.

I taught myself to be a parent and a student at the same time, helping my siblings with their homework while I did my own, getting stuff done early when the house was quiet. School was where I could escape. The pages of my books didn’t judge me. The teachers didn’t complain when I was too tired to speak, and sometimes just being in a classroom was enough to gain strength from learning, maybe something I learned from my grandpa since he was a life long learner.

Everyday I think about quitting. The day I broke my back and shoulders I literally thought I might never recover. The only place I had joy was with football. I wanted to quit. I stayed in school while recovering, and stayed on top of schoolwork. I feared that if I did not keep my word, I would never change the narrative of my life days when grief forced me to carry a weight I could not lift. But I keep coming back because I believe in education. I believe in where it might take me, and most importantly I believe in where it might take my family.

I’m not entering college as an escape, and I’m not on a mission to get a degree. I want to study psychology and become a person who helps others overcome circumstances. I want to show my siblings that the beginning doesn’t dictate the ending, and remind the young boy I once was—all cozied up on a couch with a flashlight and a book—that he was always meant to do it.

I've learned that strength is not always loud. Sometimes, strength looks like getting up early enough before the sun even comes up to make everyone lunches. Sometimes it looks like studying in the morning when it’s finally quiet. Sometimes I’m not sure if I’m strong at all but I know that some day it will all be worth it. I have the weight of my family's hope on my shoulders and I carry it with pride.

This scholarship would not be just a financial burden relieved, but it would be a doorway. A way to turn pain into purpose, because then it would all be for something. A way to make my grandfather proud, repay my mother of all her sacrifices, and to be the role model and example for my siblings.

I am not looking for an easy path, I have never had one. I am just looking to keep moving forward.

Summative Assessments

Student assignments, quizzes and exams, and participation points contribute towards a student's final score. The artifacts presented:

  • History of Computer Science Exit Ticket: the student demonstrates that they mastered the learning objectives.
  • Mock Final Exam for the Algorithms class: for obvious reasons I don't want to post the real exam on the internet, but this mock has questions similar to or from the same test bank as the real exam but with the numbers changed. Students can take the mock as many times as they like, but it is work no points. It is in that sense a formative assessment but an example of what the summative exam will look like.
  • Final Exam Item Banks: the Algorithms final exam is 25 questions, online, in-person, on school computers only with internet access limited to Canvas and the online course textbook. The questions are randomly taken from the banks per the break-down. Question order is randomized and answer choices are randomized.
  • Final Exam Question Break-Down: questions are randomly selected from the Item Banks by this break-down.
