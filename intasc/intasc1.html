InTASC Standard #1
Learner Development

The teacher understands how learners grow and develop, recognizing that patterns of learning and development vary individually within and across the cognitive, linguistic, social, emotional, and physical areas, and designs and implements developmentally appropriate and challenging learning experiences.

High School students come to computer programming classes with different levels of programming, motivations, and learning rates. Along with theory, demonstrations, and coding assignments I provide mentoring, feedback, and significant scaffolding as needed. Some only need coding opportunity while others struggle and need a lot of personal attention.

Scaffolding Computer Science

Computer Science – Programming in C# was an introductory computer programming class for many students. The students had varying levels of background. Half the class was designated Highly Capable; many of those had previous coding experience and many were driven to learn. Many had no coding experience and needed extra help. Two had cognitive difficulties and needed scaffolding.

To begin a unit, I did a code-along demonstration of a particular concept or set of concepts. Students could observe the code displayed on screen as I wrote it and were invited to “code along”. I went slowly, talking about the concept as I did, and allowing the class to direct me as to whether they needed time to catch up. After completion, the demo code I wrote would be made available to the students, as well as the code they were expected to have written with me. They were then given an assignment that was similar to the demo but different enough to require them to apply the concepts to a new context.

While the students worked on their assignment, I made the rounds, paying special attention to the students who normally needed help, but also checking in with the others and being open to questions.

Students were also encouraged to help each other, and several students had “go-to” people they went to for help.

For the students who had cognitive difficulties, I would pull up a chair and talk them through the assignment. Usually the demo code was close enough for them to copy/paste or to follow the pattern for the new context. I would sometimes spend 20-25% of the assignment time with them, but it was also surprising to me how much they could accomplish on their own.

In retrospect, I wish I had put more comments in my code while I was writing it to serve as directions for students. That would have encouraged them to enter such comments in their code as they coded along, which would help them completing their assignment. It is also a good habit to develop in general.

Adding Challenge

A significant number of students in my programming classes were highly capable and highly motivated. The goal was to keep their interest and provide challenging learning opportunities while not overwhelming those who need more help.

My solution was to begin each lesson simply with what the students already knew, adding more to it until we reached the learning objectives. I taught theory first but then moved right into demonstrating how to write the code. The students who needed minimal instruction could jump right into doing the assignment without waiting for me to finish the lesson. While I was doing this, I threw out teasers by mentioning a coding technique that would solve a more complex problem that I could show them “later”, just to pique interest. Then as I did my rounds I would stop at the desks of hi-cap students and say, “hey, want to see something?” and show them the more advanced technique.

But these extra lessons were not only offered to the students designated hi-cap. Often, while working 1:1 with a struggling student, if things were connecting for them then along with my 1:1 recitation of the theory I’d elaborate a bit more and show them the technique, too. This made them feel special, recipients of the secret knowledge. Other students weren’t ready for it or not interested, and that was fine because it wasn’t required for the assignment.

Another strategy was to add a “Challenge” problem to an assignment. It wasn’t required, but it could earn extra points.
