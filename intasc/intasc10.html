InTASC Standard #10
Leadership and Collaboration

The candidate seeks appropriate leadership roles and opportunities to take responsibility for student learning, to collaborate with learners, families, colleagues, other school professionals, and community members to ensure learner growth, and to advance the profession.

There are many forms of leadership. Leadership is thinking beyond yourself to what is good for the your school and community. Having a vision for where you want to go, planning how to get there, and inspiring others to join you is clearly leadership, and we think of such people as charasmatic. But there are other types of leaders, and here's the key: leadership can be distributed. Some provide vital expertise and services. Some act in an advisory capacity, stepping back giving perspective. Even just showing up faithfully is leadership, because without a cadre of reliable, supportive volunteers every non-profit organization would fail.

Standard #10 is also about collaboration with students and cross-discipline with colleagues. There are many natural connections that add interest and make the learner's education more well-rounded.

Hunt the Wumpus

Spring quarter the students in my C# classes were assigned into development teams to create a computer game for the “Hunt the Wumpus” competition sponsored by Microsoft. The students were assigned areas of code to create, such as cave system, score board, player, and movement. They had to learn to work together to make it happen, with me, my mentor, and the Microsoft volunteers who came twice a week acting as advisors.

I taught the students about source control, how to create a code repository on GitHub, and how to merge code so that multiple developers can work together in the same repository. I gave advice, montiored progress, and helped to debug and sometimes detangle failed merges. Students were required to add me as a repository collaborator.

On the big day we took our classes to Microsoft for the judging competition with several other schools competing. Three of our eight teams brought home trophies, one of which is also presented as an artifact. Other artifacts presented are a screenshot of a GitHub repository and a collaboration invite.

Social Studies

History of Computer Science PowerPoint presentation: I give this lecture on the first day of all my computer science classes (HTML, C#, Java, Algorithms) because it is important for students to understand where we are in history, the contributions of the pioneers, and what new challenges their generation will work on. It's also important to see no matter who you are that important contributions were made by people who look like you.

Scholarship Essay

This last year I also taught Personal Finance, including a unit on Paying for College. One of the lessons I developed was on winning scholarships, with an assigned scholarship essay. Students were taught that the elements of a winning scholarhip essay are:

  • Core Values
  • Personal Insights
  • Vulnerability
  • Craft
  • A tie-in to your educational and career goals
  • How you plan to use your education to give back

Reading and Diversity

For my Children’s Literature class this summer, we created posters to promote diversity in reading, keeping in mind our school’s demographics and the interests of our students. At my host school, Asians are the largest minority group at 20%; in my Computer Science classes it’s over to 50%. My CS students are naturally interested in science and science fiction so my theme was Exploration with motifs of space, the oceans, and computers. The resources represented include graphic novels, chapter books, fiction and non-fiction, photo essay books, magazines, and websites. These posters would be of interest to my students, promote reading, and not look at all out of place in a computer science classroom.

Toastmasters Club

I've been a Toastmaster for five years. I've been an officer in multiple roles, including club president and area director. As an area director, I visited the clubs assigned to me and worked with them to acheive success. We were awarded President's Distinguished Area, which is the highest level award. We were the only area in the district to acheive that status. My artifact is a screenshot of the notification email from our district director.

The computer science curriculum in most colleges focuses on technical skills, yet communication and leadership skills are critical in every career. Toastmasters can bridge that gap. While one must be over 18 to be a member of Toastmasters, high school students can participate in the Youth Leadership Program. It is my desire to sponsor one at my school.
